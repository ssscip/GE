MONITORING OUR PLANET'S EVOLUTION

A quarter-century of global environmental observation and analysis: 2000-2025

Latest Events

Australian Black Summer (2019-2020)

Unprecedented bushfires devastated over 46 million acres of land, causing catastrophic damage to ecosystems and wildlife. Satellite imagery revealed the true scale of this disaster, with smoke plumes visible from space.

Hurricane Katrina (2005)

One of the most destructive Atlantic hurricanes in U.S. history, captured in detail by NASA's satellites. The storm surge and flooding transformed our understanding of coastal vulnerability and disaster response.

Eyjafjallajökull Eruption (2010)

Iceland's volcanic eruption disrupted global air travel for weeks. Satellite monitoring provided crucial data about ash cloud movements, revolutionizing volcanic activity tracking systems.

Arctic Sea Ice Minimum (2012)

Satellites recorded the lowest Arctic sea ice extent in recorded history, marking a critical moment in climate change monitoring. This event became a powerful symbol of global warming's impact.

About the Project

Global Monitoring 2000-2025 is an innovative data visualization project developed for the NASA Space Apps Challenge, showcasing a quarter-century of Earth observation data. By harnessing the power of NASA's extensive satellite network, we've created a comprehensive timeline of our planet's environmental evolution.

Our project combines data from multiple NASA missions, including Terra, Aqua, and the Suomi NPP satellites, to create an unprecedented view of Earth's changing face. Through interactive visualizations and detailed analysis, we highlight significant environmental events, climate patterns, and long-term trends that have shaped our world.

This platform serves as both a scientific resource and a public education tool, making complex environmental data accessible to everyone. By presenting 25 years of observations in an engaging format, we aim to foster understanding of our planet's dynamic systems and inspire action for environmental preservation.
